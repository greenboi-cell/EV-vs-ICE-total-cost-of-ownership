Executive Summary & Key Findings

The Ather 450X EV becomes the cheaper option from Year 2 onward for average commuters (30 km/day), saving ₹72,212 over five years[cite: 2]. For heavy commuters (50 km/day), break-even occurs as early as Year 1.5, with total savings of ₹1,41,248[cite: 2]. Light commuters (10 km/day) only marginally recover the EV premium by Year 5, saving just ₹3,177[cite: 2]. The financial advantage of EVs scales directly with daily usage[cite: 2].

Upfront Costs & Policy Context

The transition is heavily supported by government subsidies. Combined subsidies total ₹32,848, reducing the Ather's effective on-road cost to ₹1,27,866[cite: 1].

  • Delhi EV Policy 2024–30: 100% road tax waiver plus a ₹20,000 state purchase subsidy (Year 1)[cite: 1].
  • PM E-DRIVE Scheme: Features a ₹10,900 Crore central outlay, providing a ₹5,000 subsidy per eligible two-wheeler[cite: 1].
  • Delhi's 2028 ICE Mandate: Requires 100% electric two-wheeler registrations from April 2028, creating a structural resale value risk for petrol scooters purchased in 2026[cite: 1].

After factoring in these subsidies and the Year 1 insurance, the upfront premium of the Ather 450X over the Honda Activa 6G is ₹38,341[cite: 1]. This premium must be recovered through the EV's significantly lower running costs[cite: 1].

Strategic Outlook

For any Delhi commuter riding 30 km or more daily, the Ather 450X is already the financially superior choice in 2026[cite: 1]. The EV transition economics depend on the total cost of ownership, not the upfront price[cite: 1]. However, heavy commuters should remain aware of non-modelled risks, such as potential post-warranty battery replacement costs ranging from ₹49,000 to ₹84,500[cite: 1].
